intersection of sphere and plane


Theorem.  The intersection curve of a sphere and a plane is a circle.

Proof.  We prove the theorem without the equation of the sphere.  Let c be the intersection curve, r the radius of the sphere and O⁢Q be the distance of the centre O of the sphere and the plane.  If P is an arbitrary point of c, then O⁢P⁢Q is a right triangle.  By the Pythagorean theorem,

P⁢Q=ϱ=r2-O⁢Q2= constant.

Thus any point of the curve c is in the plane at a distance ϱ from the point Q, whence c is a circle.

Remark.  There are two special cases of the intersection of a sphere and a plane:  the empty set of points (O⁢Q>r) and a single point (O⁢Q=r); these of course are not curves.  In the former case one usually says that the sphere does not intersect the plane, in the latter one sometimes calls the common point a zero circle (it can be thought a circle with radius 0).
