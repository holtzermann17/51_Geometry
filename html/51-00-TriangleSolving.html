triangle solving


Let us consider skew-angled triangles.  If one knows three parts of a triangle, among which at least one side, then the other parts may be calculated by using the law of sines and the law of cosines.  We distinguish four cases:

  1. 1.

    ASA.  Known two angles and one side, e.g. α, β, c.  Other parts:

    γ=180∘-(α+β),a=c⁢sin⁡αsin⁡γ,b=c⁢sin⁡βsin⁡γ
  2. 2.

    SSS.  Known all sides a, b, c.  The angles are obtained from

    cos⁡α=b2+c2-a22⁢b⁢c,cos⁡β=c2+a2-b22⁢c⁢a,cos⁡γ=a2+b2-c22⁢a⁢b.
  3. 3.

    SAS.  Known two sides and the angle between them, e.g. b, c, α.  Other parts from

    a2=b2+c2-2⁢b⁢c⁢cos⁡α,sin⁡β=b⁢sin⁡αa,sin⁡γ=c⁢sin⁡αa
  4. 4.

    SSA.  Known two sides and the angle of one of them, e.g. a, b, α.  Other parts are gotten from

    sin⁡β=b⁢sin⁡αa,γ=180∘-(α+β),c=a⁢sin⁡γsin⁡α.

    Since the SSA criterion alone does not prove congruence, it is not surprising that there may not always be a single solution for β here. In fact, if the first equation gives  sin⁡β>1,  then the situation is impossible and the triangle does not exist.  If the equation gives  sin⁡β<1,  one gets two distinct values of β; an acute β1 and an obtuse  β2=180∘-β1.  If in this case  β1>α,  then there are two different triangles as , but if  β1≤α,  then there is only one triangle.
