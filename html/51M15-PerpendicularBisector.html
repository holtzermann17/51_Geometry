perpendicular bisector


Let A⁢B¯ be a line segment in a plane (we are assuming the Euclidean plane). A bisector of A⁢B¯ is any line that passes through the midpoint of A⁢B¯. A perpendicular bisector of A⁢B¯ is a bisector that is perpendicular to A⁢B¯.

It is an easy exercise to show that a line ℓ is a perpendicular bisector of A⁢B¯ iff every point lying on ℓ is equidistant from A and B. From this, one concludes that the perpendicular bisector of a line segment is always unique.

A basic way to construct the perpendicular bisector ℓ given a line segment A⁢B¯ using the standard ruler and compass construction is as follows:

  1. 1.

    use a compass to draw the circle C1 centered at point A with radius the length of A⁢B¯, by fixing one end of the compass at A and the movable end at B,

  2. 2.

    similarly, draw the circle C2 centered at B with the same radius as above, with the compass fixed at B and movable at A,

  3. 3.

    C1 and C2 intersect at two points, say P,Q (why?)

  4. 4.

    with a ruler, draw the line P⁢Q↔=ℓ,

  5. 5.

    then ℓ is the perpendicular bisector of A⁢B¯.

(Note: we assume that there is always an ample supply of compasses and rulers of varying sizes, so that given any positive real number r, we can find a compass that opens wider than r and a ruler that is longer than r).

One of the most common use of perpendicular bisectors is to find the center of a circle constructed from three points in a Euclidean plane:

Given three non collinear points X,Y,Z in a Euclidean plane, let C be the unique circle determined by X,Y,Z. Then the center of C is located at the intersection of the perpendicular bisectors of X⁢Y¯ and Y⁢Z¯.
